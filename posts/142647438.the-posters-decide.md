*It‚Äôs a tough call to choose which ChatGPT wrapper to invest in. They‚Äôre so different.*

Personally, 2020 is not the year I would choose to go back and relive, but earlier this week, the United States decided that is what it wanted, and [nominated Joe Biden and Donald Trump](https://www.nytimes.com/2024/03/12/us/politics/trump-republican-nomination.html) to run for president again.¬†

They both won their respective elections because they were ostensibly the most preferred candidates by American voters. However, [there is a theory](https://press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/P/bo5921600.html) among political scientists that voters have less say in who wins presidential primaries than it appears. Nominees are chosen by party insiders and Washington power brokers, who typically coalesce around a preferred candidate, and then court donors, secure key endorsements, and recruit talented staffers on that candidate‚Äôs behalf. These efforts tilt the playing field towards their candidate, by giving them material advantages like more money and a better team, and by creating media narratives‚Äîthree Super Tuesday governors all endorsed [Bill Pullman](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3_QSJyJaeD4)! Key donors are having second thoughts about supporting [Billy Bob Thorton](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iA3WLXRuC9E&t=171s)!‚Äîthat define one candidate as a frontrunner and others as fringe.¬†

Importantly, this isn‚Äôt some conspiracy theory about stealing elections, or smoke-filled secret societies, or the Illuminati; voters still vote, and their votes still count. It‚Äôs a theory of *influence*: Parties have influencers; those influencers have money, followers, and connections; those resources can be used to alter public opinion. Sometimes, influencers will disagree; sometimes, their [influence isn‚Äôt enough to sway voters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Republicans_who_opposed_the_Donald_Trump_2016_presidential_campaign); sometimes, voters sway influencers‚Äô opinions. Still, the argument goes, we shouldn‚Äôt see elections as decided by an entirely free market‚Äîand the more the insiders agree, the less say the everyday voter has.[^1]¬†

But this is all a Washington thing; this blog is about Silicon Valley; Silicon Valley is not like Washington. A few months ago, Bill Gurley, a prominent venture capitalist and the personification of Silicon Valley swagger, [said in a thundering presentation](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=F9cO3-MLHOM&t=1424s) that ‚ÄúSilicon Valley is 2,851 miles away from Washington, and as these people put their eyes towards us, I would state the following: The reason Silicon Valley has been so successful is that it‚Äôs so fucking far away from Washington, DC.‚Äù The line got a standing ovation. Here, we don‚Äôt have cabals; we have free-market capitalism. There is no ‚Äúparty‚Äù that ‚Äúdecides;‚Äù there are no monied superdelegates; there is no [invisible primary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invisible_primary). There is only the invisible hand‚Äîusers who choose, customers who buy, [ideas whose time has come](https://twitter.com/Eric_BIGfund/status/1766807871843393854).[^2] This is a meritocracy, [if Washington will let us keep it](https://www.nps.gov/articles/000/constitutionalconvention-september17.htm).

Or at least that‚Äôs a nice story? Because Silicon Valley sure seems to be driven by the exact same dynamic that people say drives presidential primaries: Elite insiders choosing who gets money and who doesn‚Äôt, influencing who can hire and who can‚Äôt, and tilting markets and (social) media narratives in favor of their preferred technologies, companies, and founders. In other words, in the way we chose our successes, Silicon Valley doesn‚Äôt seem very far from Washington at all.

# Same medium, subtle differences

When people ask me about the very early days of working at Mode, I often say that [I wrote articles for our corporate blog](https://mixpanel.com/blog/benn-stancil-mode-thoughtspot-newsletter/#:~:text=So%20the%20thing%20that%20I%20started%20doing%20was%20writing%20blog%20posts%20for%20Mode%E2%80%99s%20corporate%20blog.). This isn't entirely true. The initial posts that I wrote weren‚Äôt actually published on Mode‚Äôs website, but on a blogging platform called [Svbtle](https://svbtle.com/). I wrote my first blog post as a Mode employee two days after we started the company,[^3] which was well before we had a corporate brand‚Äîand therefore, well before we had a website on which we could host a blog. So we had to choose a third-party platform instead.

Back then, the two up-and-coming options were Svbtle and Medium.[^4] As products, they were [basically identical](https://medium.com/@erickuhn19/medium-vs-svbtle-25dbaa145402)‚Äîboth were very easy to set up; both embraced the crisp millennial minimalism aesthetic that was irresistible in 2013;[^5] both came with nifty charts that would show me the dozens (dozens!) of people who read what I wrote; both had nascent distribution and discovery features that would, in theory, push my posts out to new readers via some algorithmic recommendation engine. I chose Svbtle, and passed on Medium, for none of these reasons. I chose it because [it was still invite-only](https://blog.svbtle.com/open-for-everyone) and using it made the egomaniac in me feel special, and because Svtle‚Äôs ‚Äúlike‚Äù feature‚Äîcalled Kudos[^6]‚Äîwas fun.[^7]¬†

Most of Silicon Valley‚Äôs elite chose Medium. It raised money from some of the industry's most followed venture capitalists, including Greylock‚Äôs [Josh Elman](https://joshelman.medium.com/), a16z‚Äôs [Ben Horowitz](https://medium.com/@bhorowitz), and Google Ventures‚Äô [M.G. Siegler](https://mgs.blog/), all of whom began posting on Medium. Though Svbtle had [fans](https://techcrunch.com/2014/01/28/low-overhead-blogging-platform-svbtle-opens-up-to-all/) and [notable backers](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/svbtle) of its own, most prominent writers in tech‚Äî[Hunter Walk](https://hunterwalk.medium.com/), [Julie Zhuo](https://joulee.medium.com/), [David Heinemeier Hansson](https://medium.com/@dhh), [Jason Fried](https://medium.com/@jasonfried), [Jason Calcanis](https://medium.com/@jason), [Stewart Butterfield](https://medium.com/@stewart), [Anil Dash](https://medium.com/@anildash), [Chris Sacca](https://medium.com/@sacca), [Dustin Moskovitz](https://medium.com/@moskov), [Brad Feld](https://medium.com/@bfeld), even [Bill Gurley](https://medium.com/@bgurley)‚Äîcoalesced around Medium. Within two years, [people were declaring](https://robertnealan.com/is-self-hosted-blogging-dead/) Svbtle dead, and had accepted Medium as Silicon Valley‚Äôs default blogging platform.¬†

Of course, products succeed and fail for lots of reasons. We‚Äôll never know exactly why Medium won and Svbtle lost. Maybe Medium‚Äôs editor was a little better; maybe its viral distribution loops were a little tighter. Maybe Medium was right [to hire their own writers](https://techcrunch.com/2013/04/17/medium-acquires-matter-as-long-form-journalism-site-joins-evan-williams-startup/). Maybe bombastic VC grandstanding is better paired with an elegant [serif](https://ev.medium.com/welcome-to-medium-9e53ca408c48) than austere [sans serif](https://blog.svbtle.com/open-for-everyone). Maybe Svbtle was [too exclusive](https://www.garron.me/en/blog/svbtle-vs-medium.html) for too long. Maybe [product science](https://medium.com/@sall/product-scientist-ffd1ae846172) really works. I don‚Äôt know.¬†

But as someone who was publishing on Svbtle at the time, it felt like the only thing that mattered was Medium's momentum‚Äîand specifically, its momentum among people who had big existing followings. When I shared my posts, people periodically asked me why I was using Svbtle. Wasn‚Äôt Medium the thing everyone was using now? Could I [not get an invite to it yet](https://techcrunch.com/2013/10/25/twitter-co-founder-evan-williams-blogging-platform-medium-opens-signups-to-all/)? If you used Medium, you were a respectable writer with an independent voice. If you knew about it, you were in the club. IYKYK.

Very little of this perception came from people‚Äôs lived experiences of writing on Medium, and even less of it came from people who had tried both products and found the nuances of Medium to be more effective or more agreeable than Svbtle. Medium‚Äôs momentum came from important people in Silicon Valley posting on it and posting about it on Twitter, and from the rest of us following them. That doesn‚Äôt discount the quality of Medium‚Äôs product‚Äîit was good, and Silicon Valley‚Äôs influencers surely talked about it in part because it was good. But [it feels incomplete to tell the story](https://mariusmasalar.me/farewell-to-svbtle-fb9ca1da38db) of Medium‚Äôs success, and of Svbtle‚Äôs failure, by just talking about the product. Surely, the party also played a role.¬†

# The emperor occasionally experiments with editing

If I‚Äôm really honest about it, watching this unfold probably had an overly formative effect on me and my sense of how Silicon Valley works. But still, once you see it‚Äîonce you see elite opinion become public perception, and public perception become at least some sort of reality‚Äîit‚Äôs hard not to see it everywhere. There was a moment in 2014 [when Slack was suddenly the only product](https://www.cbinsights.com/research/slack-app-store-jump-a16z/) anyone could talk about. There was a moment in 2016 when Airtable, a fairly confusing product that wasn‚Äôt easily identifiable as anything, was broadly hailed as the new way to build everything. There was a moment in 2017 when VCs and startup founders were obsessed with‚Äîand obsessed with talking about‚ÄîSuperhuman.¬†

There was another moment like this earlier this week. A small startup called Cognition Labs [launched Devin](https://twitter.com/cognition_labs/status/1767548763134964000), an AI agent that can develop software applications on its own. The video blew up, with [dozens](https://twitter.com/patrickc/status/1767603551927242809) [of](https://twitter.com/dhh/status/1767718092250235073) [notable](https://twitter.com/karpathy/status/1767598414945292695) [Silicon](https://twitter.com/levelsio/status/1767580614234906820) [Valley](https://twitter.com/petergyang/status/1767662981540950163) [influencers](https://twitter.com/AravSrinivas/status/1767582756291387484) posting about how Devin was a remarkable leap forward for both AI and for software development. Within a few hours of the announcement, [people were already suggesting](https://twitter.com/thogge/status/1767592255433617649) that it might be the next *$100* billion company, which is 25 percent more than [even OpenAI is currently worth](https://www.nytimes.com/2024/02/16/technology/openai-artificial-intelligence-deal-valuation.html).

Which, maybe! A hundred billion seems high, but I don‚Äôt know! Cognition CEO Scott Wu [can apparently solve impossible math questions](https://twitter.com/jfbrly/status/1767653596957642879) in less time than it takes most of us to *read *impossible math questions, so if you have to put a bet down on something, I guess Cognition is as good as anything.¬†

What seems notable, though, is I‚Äôm not saying that because I‚Äôve used Devin, which is still behind a closed beta, nor am I saying it that because the launch video was *that* unique or impressive. There are tons of companies building AI agents like Devin, and [you can never fully trust a pre-recorded demo](https://techcrunch.com/2023/12/07/googles-best-gemini-demo-was-faked/). Instead, my belief in the potential of Cognition comes entirely from other people‚Äôs belief in the potential of Cognition‚Äîwhich was, *at least to some degree*, [astroturfed](https://twitter.com/patrickc/status/1767603551927242809) [by](https://twitter.com/saranormous/status/1767564859603030352) [Cognition‚Äôs](https://twitter.com/eglyman/status/1767556597348470813) [investors](https://twitter.com/absoluttig/status/1767556549445341263) and [PR team](https://twitter.com/lulumeservey/status/1767657576106275041).

The point here isn‚Äôt that the emperor has no clothes, or even that they don‚Äôt have $100 billion clothes. The point is that [nobody has seen the emperor](https://variety.com/2024/politics/global/where-is-kate-middleton-altered-photo-controversy-1235940634/). Investors have, and we‚Äôve [seen curated demos](https://twitter.com/KensingtonRoyal/status/1767135566645092616?s=20), and the emperor is very good at math. But the clothes are still invite-only. And yet, all at once, the entire internet began telling the same stories of their wonder.

Imagine that you didn‚Äôt see the Devin demo because a Silicon Valley celebrity tweeted about it. Imagine that the video only had a few thousand views. Imagine that you found it on the second page of Product Hunt, on a slow Thursday afternoon. Imagine it had fifteen upvotes and a lonely comment from the ‚Äúmaker,‚Äù saying they were very excited to share their project with the world. Imagine that the thing itself was exactly the same, but there was no party and no posting around it.¬†

Would it be impressive? Would you think anything of it? Would you want to invest? Would you roll your eyes at what was probably the fourth AI bot that you saw on your bored scroll through Product Hunt?¬†

I really don‚Äôt know. I doubt I would‚Äôve thought that it was any different [than the other belts I‚Äôve seen](https://youtu.be/Ja2fgquYTCg?si=YuDLxIpLKtbAB2za&t=26). Which may well be *wrong*‚ÄîDevin really might be different, and in ten years, Devin may be building all of our websites, and our phone apps, and even future Devins. But without the hype, I don‚Äôt think Devin and its five minute sizzle reel would register to me as anything that was particularly noteworthy.¬†

But *with* the hype, I would invest in it? Partly, perhaps, because [I‚Äôve seen the light](https://twitter.com/lulumeservey/status/1767730891957116938), but more because Silicon Valley‚Äôs influencers‚Äîthe party‚Äîhave decided that Devin is important. Cognition can now easily court investors, secure key partnerships, and recruit talented staffers. The playing field is tilted in their favor; they have material advantages like more money and a better team, and a media narrative that defines them as a frontrunner in a very noisy space.[^8]¬†And once you‚Äôre on the ballot, you‚Äôve got a shot.

# From a pile of ‚Äústuff‚Äù

On one hand, all this is very silly to say. Of course this is how Silicon Valley works. It's how everything works. Party elites don‚Äôt influence presidential primaries because of some unique culture of cronyism in Washington DC; they influence primaries because social systems have hierarchies, hierarchies have people at the top, and the people beneath them pay a lot of attention to what the people above them do. If Taylor Swift likes Travis Kelce, [we all like Travis Kelce](https://www.nfl.com/news/chiefs-te-travis-kelce-jersey-sales-jump-nearly-400-following-taylor-swift-appea). If Oscar de la Renta does cerulean, [we all do cerulean](https://artdepartmental.com/blog/devil-wears-prada-cerulean-monologue). If the president says it‚Äôs a revolution, [we storm the Capitol](https://twitter.com/hunterw/status/1346919171595137025?lang=en). If engineers who work for OpenAI say that Devin will transform software development, we tweet memes about how Devin is five years from taking our jobs. If important people post about something a lot, we start to post about it too‚Äîthat is not a novel point to make.

On the other hand, it still seems useful to say? The conversations about these dynamics in Silicon Valley tend to exist on the poles. There are conspiracy theorists on one side, who say that [Silicon Valley is controlled by mob bosses and hitmen](https://www.businessinsider.com/bolt-ceo-calls-stripe-ycombinator-mob-bosses-twitter-silicon-valley-2022-1), and there are [heads-down hustle heroes](https://twitter.com/mwseibel/status/1592885186416738306) on the other, who say that the only thing that matters is what you build.¬†

The reality is somewhere in the middle. Investors and tech twitter personalities aren‚Äôt all in one group chat, masterfully pulling our strings in unison, but they aren‚Äôt external observers either. In that, Silicon Valley isn‚Äôt exempt from the same dynamic that drives every industry: There are influencers, and there are the influenced. Though companies ultimately live and die on their own merits‚Äîin recent years, [Medium‚Äôs road has been rocky](https://techcrunch.com/2023/10/12/medium-profitability-2024-interview/)‚Äîthe party decides which companies win the early-stage primaries.[^9] And our predictions about breakthrough technologies and breakout successes aren‚Äôt the product of lived experiences and independent analysis. They are [selected for us](https://artdepartmental.com/blog/devil-wears-prada-cerulean-monologue/#:~:text=However%2C%20that%20blue%20represents%20millions%20of%20dollars%20of%20countless%20jobs%2C%20and%20it%E2%80%99s%20sort%20of%20comical%20how%20you%20think%20that%20you%E2%80%99ve%20made%20a%20choice%20that%20exempts%20you%20from%20the%20fashion%20industry%20when%2C%20in%20fact%2C%20you%E2%80%99re%20wearing%20a%20sweater%20that%20was%20selected%20for%20you%20by%20the%20people%20in%20this%20room%E2%80%A6%20from%20a%20pile%20of%20%E2%80%9Cstuff.%E2%80%9D).¬†
