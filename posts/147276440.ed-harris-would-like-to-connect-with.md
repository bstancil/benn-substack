Here‚Äôs something I‚Äôve wondered for a while: If you became famous on a social network that was full of bots that were programmed to treat you like an A-list celebrity, would you use it?¬†¬†

Like, you post a single emoji and a selfie on Truman, the new social network from Meta that looks indistinguishable from Twitter or Threads. It gets [three million likes](https://x.com/BTS_twt/status/1353536893787357184). Not artificially, where the like counter goes up on its own; three million different bots, who are also interacting with each other, actually click the like button. You post a dumb joke; it‚Äôs [shared 800,000 times](https://x.com/elonmusk/status/1519480761749016577). You [wade into politics](https://x.com/charli_xcx/status/1815182384066707861); everyone starts talking about what you said; important people are debating each other in your replies. Someone makes a remix of your post; [four million people watch it](https://x.com/ryanlong03/status/1808510079382982870). Your posts become memes; trends; hashtags; a verb. You get thousands of new followers, every day. People start mentioning you in conversations that you aren‚Äôt a part of; they want your opinions; they wonder what you think; they develop a parasocial relationship with you. Cute people slide into your DMs. Truman is an entire world‚Äôs water cooler; everyone is there; it is as sprawling and endless as Twitter‚Äîtrillions of interactions, billions of people, [eight million stories](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=vk6014HuxcE&t=105s)‚Äîbut here, you are one of its [main characters](https://x.com/maplecocaine/status/1080665226410889217). When Buffett speaks, [the market reacts](https://www.usatoday.com/story/money/markets/2017/02/16/buffett-effect-buy-what-he-buys/97965132/). When Kendrick drops, [we dissect every lyric](https://www.tiktok.com/@doloayers/video/7392248632436690218). And when you speak, legions of avatars like and respond.[^1]

But none of it is ‚Äúreal.‚Äù There are no human beings on the other side of your screen. When you log out of Truman and step into some other world‚Äîinto your living room with your family; out at an actual, physical coffee shop or bar; on other social networks, populated by an entirely different set of accounts‚Äînothing crosses the chasm. What happens in your Truman stays on your Truman, and what happens in everyone else‚Äôs Truman stays in theirs.

If you could join that social network‚Äîthat matrix, in which [you‚Äôre rich, someone important, like an actor](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_Wgn0KlSHl4&t=54s)‚Äîwould you? Would you post on it? Would you feel good when the bots liked what you said?

What if every other bot on Truman was its own unique AI agent? What if there were a billion of them, interacting with each other, having millions of conversations about things that aren‚Äôt you, and you were one of a few hundred popular accounts that they followed? What if they were all trained to have their own personalities? What if each of them was designed to mimic the personality of a real person, and to act like that person acted? What if it wasn't a simulation of a social network, but a simulation of an entire world, as seen through a social network?

What if the AI agents weren‚Äôt explicitly told to like you or your posts, and were instead just instructed to *read* them, and respond in whatever way the human that they represented would? What if Truman only gave you an audience‚Äîmillions of engaged followers‚Äîbut you had to earn their admiration?

What if there was only one Truman network? What if Meta could only afford to run one billion-bot social network, and you won a special lottery to be the only person at the center of it? What if your popularity on Truman was unique, because you were the only person in the world who was on Truman?

What if other people could join your Truman network? What if it was populated by AI agents that followed you, but it was also populated by real people who followed you too? What if you couldn‚Äôt tell how many of each there were, and you didn‚Äôt know who was who? [What if the bots were dogs?](https://www.smithsonianmag.com/smart-news/the-most-reprinted-new-yorker-cartoon-breaks-record-at-auction-for-a-single-panel-comic-180983135/)

What if there were no bots at all, and Truman was instead full of people who were *paid* to read your posts? What if you weren‚Äôt interacting with AI agents that were programmed with code, but with people who had been programmed with money?¬†

What if the people weren‚Äôt actually paid, but felt *professionally obligated* to like your posts? What if their core directive was commercial *quid pro quo*? What if Truman was an insular game of artificial influence, powered by an unspoken understanding that the whole thing is a ouroboros, a circular reference error, a corporate Klein bottle of content, a place where people promoted everyone else‚Äôs posts in hopes that it would somehow help promote theirs? What if the people on Truman were real, but their motivations were manufactured?¬†

What if Truman wasn't named after a [fake character](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dlnmQbPGuls) who lived at the center of a fake world, but was instead called something more plausibly genuine, like The Hub, or IndustryIQ, or WorkLife?¬†

What if it was called LinkedIn?¬†

# Social-affect capitalism

Ten years ago, LinkedIn, which was bought by Microsoft in 2016, was a r√©sum√© and a rolodex.[^2] It was a career fair for tryhards, a website that you never logged into unless you were either looking for a job, looking to fill a job, or looking up how old someone was.[^3] Now, it‚Äôs a true social network, for work. There are feeds; there are posts; there are likes and followers and influencers. And while [Microsoft shares very little](https://www.microsoft.com/investor/reports/ar23/index.html#:~:text=All%20up%2C%20LinkedIn%E2%80%99s%20revenue%20surpassed%20%2415%20billion%20for%20the%20first%20time%20this%20fiscal%20year%2C%20a%20testament%20to%20how%20mission%20critical%20the%20platform%20has%20become%20to%20help%20more%20than%20950%20million%20members%20connect%2C%20learn%2C%20sell%2C%20and%20get%20hired.) about how many people use LinkedIn, it seems to be having a prolonged moment of popularity. It‚Äôs no longer a passive professional portfolio that gets updated once every few years, but a place where people post regularly, and a website‚Äîand app!‚Äîthat we idly scroll through like we used to scroll through Facebook 15 years ago.[^4]

But calling LinkedIn a social network for work probably isn‚Äôt specific enough. Work is on other social networks too: TikTok is where we [complain about our work](https://www.tiktok.com/@joshrosen_/video/7358105645918260523); Twitter is where we get in [food fights about our work](https://x.com/anothercohen/status/1816338693575368755). LinkedIn is for‚Ä¶advertising our work? It‚Äôs our work lives, oversaturated in [Lo-Fi](https://www.animatron.com/blog/18-best-instagram-filters/#_Toc2043751548). Nearly everything on LinkedIn is either a press release or a corporate blog post for our personal brand: ‚ÄúOfficial‚Äù announcements of job changes; sharing ‚Äúmajor news‚Äù about our employers‚Äô moves eight pixels to the northeast, and across some contrived equator, on the Gartner Magic Quadrant; [AI-authored appreciation posts](https://www.npr.org/2024/07/30/nx-s1-5056201/google-olympics-ai-ad) for the hardworking team that hosted a webinar; 300-word algorithmically-optimized stories about the best advice we ever got, or a recent moment of gratitude, or some other answer to the question, ‚Äú[What is your biggest weakness?](https://www.tiktok.com/@hopstair/video/7225192988585102619)‚Äù¬†

On one hand, I get it. There are [950 million wallets](https://www.microsoft.com/investor/reports/ar23/index.html#:~:text=All%20up%2C%20LinkedIn%E2%80%99s%20revenue%20surpassed%20%2415%20billion%20for%20the%20first%20time%20this%20fiscal%20year%2C%20a%20testament%20to%20how%20mission%20critical%20the%20platform%20has%20become%20to%20help%20more%20than%20950%20million%20members%20connect%2C%20learn%2C%20sell%2C%20and%20get%20hired.) on LinkedIn. Many of us want to sell them stuff. If you can get famous on LinkedIn‚Äîwhich you can, mostly by posting on LinkedIn‚Äîyou can sell them stuff [on easy mode](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YErOtGMgTNg&t=4258s). Post about something relentlessly enough, and people will eventually buy it. Cringe is a category creator.¬†

On the other hand, this sort of social-affect capitalism‚Äî[commercials, presented like conversations](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6U4-KZSoe6g)‚Äîmakes LinkedIn an awfully strange place. Unlike other social media networks, nothing on LinkedIn is spontaneous. No post is just a thought, or the beginning of a genuine discussion. Instead, everything feels planned and scheduled; every interaction is something between politely professional and suspiciously enthusiastic; every message is awkwardly formatted to be maximally viral.[^5] The only two emotions on LinkedIn are bittersweet endings and very exciting beginnings. It‚Äôs not socializing; it‚Äôs editorial calendar crosstalk.[^6]¬†

What‚Äôs even weirder, however, is how isolated LinkedIn is. It‚Äôs a vault, locked on both sides. If something blows up on [Twitter](https://www.nytimes.com/2024/07/23/style/khive-kamala-harris-supporters.html) or [TikTok](https://www.nytimes.com/2024/07/14/arts/music/eminem-death-of-slim-shady-gen-z-canceled.html), it crosses over into group chats, into other social networks, into the *New York Times*, on to TV, and into conversations at parties and in people‚Äôs homes. Twitter isn‚Äôt real life, as they say, but the two [are definitely intertwined](https://www.nytimes.com/2020/02/19/opinion/twitter-debates-real-life.html).

But what goes viral on LinkedIn‚Äîlike [this post](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/morganmacdermaid_i-still-receive-resumes-every-week-that-activity-7208894448435347456-I1o0/), which was [viewed five million times](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7209921423459393538/)‚Äîstays on LinkedIn. The only things from LinkedIn that make their way into the broader discourse are posts that are [mocked](https://www.cnbc.com/2022/08/11/ceo-posts-crying-selfie-on-linkedin-after-laying-off-employees-and-it-goes-viral-.html) for [being](https://www.independent.co.uk/life-style/linkedin-engagement-proposal-sales-internet-b2538021.html) so LinkedIn.[^7] And there is no reporting on the latest trends on LinkedIn, because there are no trends on LinkedIn. There are no trends on PR Newswire either.¬†

As rare as it is for things to leak out of LinkedIn, it seems even harder for things to break *into* LinkedIn. Elections, assassination attempts, sports and cultural phenomena‚Äînone of it breaches the LinkedIn firewall. The gyre stays tight; the centre holds; no matter what anarchy is loose in the real world, LinkedIn‚Äôs posters plow ahead with their regularly scheduled podcasts and anodyne takes. Outside of LinkedIn, we are electrified; on LinkedIn, [we‚Äôre pacified](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=NPqDIwWMtxg&t=39s). Pop culture doesn‚Äôt exist. Politics don‚Äôt exist. The debate about Silicon Valley‚Äôs rightward pivot doesn‚Äôt exist.[^8] The Olympics don't exist‚Äîunless, of course, you can spin them into a way to sell a workshop on [Connected Leadership‚Ñ¢](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/communicationexpertspeaker_olympics-leadershipdevelopment-activity-7224400864625487873-h9R9).

Moreover, nobody brings their fame *to* LinkedIn. Global stars monetize their celebrity by selling skincare products on Instagram; they feed their egos by getting attention on Twitter; they sell their souls by marketing their memecoins on TikTok. But who exploits their fame on LinkedIn? If anything, the ultimate status symbol on LinkedIn is not needing to be on LinkedIn. Elon Musk is not there. Jeff Bezos is not there. Tim Cook is not there. Oprah is, and has posted [four times](https://www.linkedin.com/in/owinfrey/recent-activity/all/) in 12 years. LeBron James, Kendrick Lamar, Billie Eilish, Taylor Swift, Stephen King, Stephen Colbert, Charli XCX, Traci Chapman, Anna Wintour, Magnus Carlson, and Griff[^9] are not there.[^10]¬†

And that‚Äôs perhaps the most damning thing of all about LinkedIn: It‚Äôs missing the important people. It‚Äôs the happy hour at a corporate conference, but all the speakers and VIPs are at more exclusive events. It‚Äôs a hollow networking event for the rest of us; the performative socializing of the riffraff. We grandstand for one another, become ‚ÄúTop Voices‚Äù by pumping out platitudes that junior people will think is smart and senior people will agree with, and hope to eventually become rich or famous enough that we‚Äôll one day get airlifted out of LinkedIn and dropped somewhere better. Because how many people *want* to be here? How many people actually use LinkedIn just to talk to other people on LinkedIn?

# Faking for likes and digital hugs

Well, maybe a lot, in a way. Because there might be another way to look at LinkedIn that is a lot more wholesome, and a bit more hopeless.

When I‚Äôve asked people why they use it, the most common answer is that it‚Äôs a good sales channel. The second most common answer is that it‚Äôs free of the political and social drama that‚Äôs typical on sites like Twitter and Instagram, and is a place to talk to professional colleagues in professional tones. In other words, it is a place where *being boring*, at least by social media standards, is still rewarded with likes and comments.¬†

I can‚Äôt help but wonder if that‚Äôs also part of the attraction of LinkedIn: It‚Äôs a social network where we can all feel popular, even in our khakis and [corporate drawl](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JFRa7Ovym8s). It‚Äôs a perpetual online work trip, where everyone feels professionally obligated to act like friends.¬†

Over the last twenty years, we‚Äôve all become addicted to our jobs and to checking our email. Our lives have moved online. We slowly erased our personalities and replaced them with our professional brands. We lost our friends to our phones, and [we are lonely](https://www.nytimes.com/2023/04/30/opinion/loneliness-epidemic-america.html).¬†

For some people, Twitter and other online communities can fill that void. But it‚Äôs hard to get popular on Twitter or Instagram‚Äîyou have to be clever, funny, and engage in the political and social drama that a lot of people seem to either want to avoid, or have no knack for.¬†

LinkedIn offers us a deal: Don‚Äôt really be yourself; don‚Äôt talk about taboo things; put up a professional headshot; feign excitement about your new role managing enterprise IoT applications on the edge.[^11] Do all that, and LinkedIn will reward you with [likes and digital hugs](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=NPqDIwWMtxg&t=114s), with messages and DMs, and with a stream of notifications that make you feel connected and important. Sure, the friends you make might be superficial; they might be there to sell you stuff,[^12] or to ‚Äúexpand their network,‚Äù or because they‚Äôre community advocates and paid evangelists for a company that‚Äôs identified you as a likely buyer. And their likes and comments might be nudges to draw attention to themselves. But a like is still a like; people are viewing your profile; when you talk, something talks back. The conversation is real, even if the motivations are more suspect.[^13] And as dozens of AI-friend apps are proving, [that‚Äôs worth something](https://techcrunch.com/2023/05/31/character-ai-the-a16z-backed-chatbot-startup-tops-1-7m-installs-in-first-week/).

Offer up your personality and a few self-promotional posts, and LinkedIn will make you feel like a celebrity. It will turn you Truman Burbank‚Äîexcept Truman got to be himself. In this social network, you have to be an actor too.¬†
